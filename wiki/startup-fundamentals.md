# Startup Fundamentals

## What is a Startup?

A startup is a young company founded to develop a unique product or service, bring it to a market, and scale it to reach many customers. Key characteristics:

- **Young and small** — Recently founded, typically small team
- **Innovation-focused** — Solving a new problem or solving existing problems in new ways
- **High growth potential** — Designed to scale rapidly
- **High risk** — Significant probability of failure, but potential for outsized returns
- **Resource-constrained** — Limited capital, limited time, limited people

**This is different from:**
- **Small business** — Generates income for owner(s), not necessarily designed to scale dramatically
- **Growth stage company** — Larger than startup, more established business model
- **Project or side hustle** — Not intended to be primary business

## The Startup Lifecycle

### Stage 1: Ideation & Validation (Pre-Launch)
- **Goal**: Validate that the problem is real and worth solving
- **Activities**: Research, customer interviews, proof-of-concept
- **Metrics**: Customer validation, problem severity, market size
- **Funding**: Founder's own money, friends & family, perhaps small grants

### Stage 2: MVP & Launch (Early Stage)
- **Goal**: Build and launch first version of product, get initial customers
- **Activities**: Product development, first sales/users, iterate based on feedback
- **Metrics**: User acquisition, retention, engagement, product-market fit signals
- **Funding**: Seed funding, angel investors, small VC rounds

### Stage 3: Growth (Growth Stage)
- **Goal**: Achieve product-market fit and scale
- **Activities**: Increase marketing/sales, expand team, optimize operations
- **Metrics**: Growth rate, unit economics, customer acquisition cost (CAC), lifetime value (LTV)
- **Funding**: Series A, B, C funding rounds

### Stage 4: Scale (Late Stage)
- **Goal**: Dominate market, prepare for profitability or exit
- **Activities**: Enter new markets, vertical integration, efficiency
- **Metrics**: Market share, profitability, unit economics optimization
- **Funding**: Later rounds, debt financing, strategic investors

### Stage 5: Exit or Stability
- **Outcomes**: IPO, acquisition, or profitable/sustainable standalone company

## Key Startup Concepts

### Product-Market Fit
The point where a startup's product satisfies strong market demand. Signs of PMF:
- Users are coming back and using the product frequently
- Word of mouth is driving significant growth
- Retention metrics are strong
- Users are actively requesting features, not just using passively
- Competitors are noticing and responding

PMF is not a moment but a continuous state. It requires ongoing validation.

### Unit Economics
The profitability of individual transactions or customers:
- **Customer Acquisition Cost (CAC)**: Cost to acquire one customer
- **Lifetime Value (LTV)**: Total profit expected from one customer
- **Ratio**: LTV should be 3x+ CAC for sustainable growth
- **Payback Period**: How long to recover CAC from revenue

### Runway
How long a company can operate with current cash and burn rate:
- **Burn rate**: Monthly cash spent (negative cash flow)
- **Runway = Cash / Burn Rate** (in months)
- **Healthy runway**: 12-18 months
- **Runway anxiety**: Below 6 months

### Pivot
A strategic change to the business when current direction is not working:
- **Product pivot**: Change the product
- **Market pivot**: Change target customer
- **Business model pivot**: Change how you make money
- **Feature pivot**: Focus on different part of product

Pivots are not failures — they're learning and adaptation. Many successful companies pivoted (Twitter was side project, Instagram was check-in app, Slack was internal tool).

### The Startup Equation
**Impact = Product × Market × Team × Timing**

All factors matter:
- **Great product, wrong market**: Low impact
- **Right market, wrong team**: Low impact
- **Good team & product, bad timing**: Low impact
- **All aligned**: Potential for explosive growth

## Types of Startups

### B2C (Business-to-Consumer)
- Selling directly to end users
- Need to reach many customers (viral growth, marketing)
- Fast feedback loops
- High churn risk if not sticky
- Examples: social networks, consumer apps, e-commerce

### B2B (Business-to-Business)
- Selling to other businesses
- Longer sales cycles, fewer customers
- Need strong product and sales
- Higher CAC but potentially higher LTV
- More predictable revenue, but slower growth
- Examples: SaaS, enterprise software, tools

### B2B2C (Business-to-Business-to-Consumer)
- Selling to businesses who sell to consumers
- Examples: payment processors, analytics platforms

### Marketplace
- Connect buyers and sellers, take commission
- Need both sides of the network
- Chicken-and-egg problem (need supply to attract demand, need demand to attract supply)
- Network effects create defensibility

### Hardware
- Building physical products
- Longer development cycles, higher capital requirements
- Manufacturing complexity
- Typically starts with limited customers, then scales

### Deep Tech / Science-Based
- Founded on scientific breakthrough or deep technical innovation
- Long development timelines, high capital requirement
- May take years before revenue
- Examples: biotech, energy, advanced materials

## Why Startups Fail

Research shows common failure reasons:
- **No market need** (42%): Built something nobody wanted
- **Ran out of cash** (29%): Burned too fast or fundraising failed
- **Wrong team** (23%): Lacked necessary skills or couldn't work together
- **Poor product** (17%): Failed to solve real problem adequately
- **Pricing issues** (10%): Charged too much or too little
- **Ignored customers** (14%): Didn't listen to user feedback
- **Poor timing** (13%): Too early or too late to market

## Why Startups Succeed

- **Found real problem** and validated with customers before building
- **Built product people love** — retention, engagement, word of mouth
- **Had right people** — complementary skills, shared vision, ability to execute
- **Focused deeply** — didn't try to do everything, maintained discipline
- **Listened to users** — regularly talked to customers, iterated based on feedback
- **Managed cash carefully** — conservative with spending, understood burn and runway
- **Adapted when needed** — pivoted when data showed current direction wasn't working
- **Persisted through setbacks** — didn't give up when things got hard

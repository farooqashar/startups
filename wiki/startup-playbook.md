# The Startup Playbook

A practical guide to building a startup from idea to product-market fit.

## Phase 1: Validate Your Idea (Weeks 1-4)

Before building anything, validate that people actually care about your idea.

### Step 1: Define the Problem
- What problem are you solving?
- Who has this problem?
- How severe is the problem?
- How do they currently solve it?

### Step 2: Interview Potential Customers
- Conduct 20-30 customer interviews (even if rough)
- Ask open-ended questions about their workflow and challenges
- Don't sell them on your idea — listen to understand
- Look for enthusiasm and confirmation that the problem is real

### Step 3: Size the Market
- Estimate how many people have this problem
- Assess if this is a market worth addressing
- Check for existing solutions and competitors

**Success criteria**: Multiple customers express genuine interest in solving this problem, willing to pay for solution

## Phase 2: Plan & Prepare (Weeks 5-8)

Define your approach and gather resources.

### Step 1: Define Your MVP (Minimum Viable Product)
- What's the smallest thing you can build that solves the core problem?
- Focus on solving 1-2 customer problems exceptionally well
- Don't include "nice-to-have" features
- MVP is a learning tool, not the final product

### Step 2: Assemble Your Team
- Do you need co-founders? What skills are missing?
- Key early roles: product/engineering, sales/marketing, business operations
- Equity splits should reflect contribution and future expectations
- Hire slow, fire fast

### Step 3: Handle Legal & Incorporation
- Incorporate (LLC, C-corp, S-corp depending on plan)
- Establish cap table and equity vesting (4-year vest, 1-year cliff standard)
- Get basic contracts reviewed
- Open business bank account

### Step 4: Plan Your First Year
- Develop simple financial model (revenue, costs, runway)
- Identify first revenue goal (if bootstrapping) or funding goal
- Create high-level timeline
- Keep it simple — plans change anyway

## Phase 3: Build & Launch (Weeks 9-16)

Build your MVP and get it in users' hands.

### Step 1: Build MVP (In Parallel with Validation)
- Keep scope tightly focused
- Use existing tools where possible (don't rebuild the wheel)
- Get to launchable version in 6-8 weeks
- Don't aim for polish — aim for solving the problem

### Step 2: Continue Customer Discovery
- While building, stay in touch with early customers
- Share prototypes and get feedback
- Iterate based on real feedback, not guesses

### Step 3: Launch
- Launch to early customers or warm audience first
- Get feedback, collect testimonials
- Be visible — launch publicly once ready

### Step 4: Set Up Basic Operations
- Analytics and metrics tracking
- Customer feedback mechanisms
- Basic support/communication channels
- Financial tracking

## Phase 4: Iterate Toward PMF (Months 3-9)

Achieve product-market fit through rapid iteration.

### The Build-Measure-Learn Cycle
1. **Build** — Ship changes based on customer feedback
2. **Measure** — Track metrics and customer responses
3. **Learn** — Extract insights, decide next steps
4. Repeat weekly or bi-weekly

### Key Activities
- Meet with users constantly (1-2x per week if possible)
- Ship updates frequently (weekly ideally)
- Track leading indicators (usage, engagement, retention)
- Conduct win/loss analysis with customers

### What PMF Looks Like
- Users are actively coming back and using regularly
- Retention curves are strong (50%+ retained after 30 days)
- Word of mouth is meaningful source of new users
- Net promoter score (NPS) is high (40+)
- Users actively request new features (not just passive use)

### If You're Not Finding PMF
- Pivot to adjacent problem
- Try different customer segment
- Modify business model (free to paid, B2B to B2C, etc.)
- Kill the project and start new one

## Phase 5: Build for Growth (Months 9+)

Once you have PMF, focus on sustainable growth.

### Growth Channels
Identify which channels work:
- **Organic/word of mouth**: If product is remarkable
- **Sales**: For B2B, direct enterprise sales
- **Content/community**: Build audience, establish thought leadership
- **Partnerships**: Distribution partnerships with complementary companies
- **Advertising**: Once you understand unit economics

### Unit Economics
- Calculate CAC (cost to acquire customer)
- Calculate LTV (lifetime value)
- Ensure LTV >> CAC (3x minimum)
- Optimize for unit economics before scaling spend

### Team & Organization
- Build department structure
- Document processes and culture
- Expand team strategically (don't hire just because you have money)

### Operational Efficiency
- Systematize core processes
- Improve customer support and retention
- Plan for scaling infrastructure

## Key Metrics to Track

### Engagement
- Daily/monthly active users (DAU/MAU)
- Frequency of use
- Feature adoption
- Session length or time spent

### Retention
- Day 1, 7, 30 retention rates
- Churn rate (% users leaving per month)
- Cohort analysis (do newer users behave differently?)

### Monetization
- Customer acquisition cost (CAC)
- Lifetime value (LTV)
- Monthly recurring revenue (MRR) or annual recurring revenue (ARR)
- Gross margin

### Growth
- Month-over-month (MoM) growth rate
- Net dollar retention (for expansion revenue)
- Viral coefficient (if applicable)

## Critical Success Factors

1. **Speed**: Move fast, don't over-plan
2. **Focus**: Do one thing well, not many things okay
3. **Customer obsession**: Stay close to customers, listen to what they say
4. **Discipline**: Stick to priorities, say no to distractions
5. **Learning**: Extract insights from every interaction, test hypotheses
6. **Resilience**: Setbacks are part of the journey, keep moving
7. **Flexibility**: Be willing to adapt when data suggests change
8. **Professionalism**: Professional execution signals quality to customers

## Red Flags

Watch for these warning signs:

- **No customer traction after 3 months** — Something isn't working
- **Lack of retention** — Product doesn't solve real problem
- **No clear revenue path** — Unclear how you'll make money
- **Team dysfunction** — Can't work together effectively
- **Running out of cash** — Miscalculated burn or can't fundraise
- **Ignoring customer feedback** — Building what you think is right, not what customers need
- **Too much pivoting** — Changing direction constantly (vs. learning and adapting)
- **Hiring too fast** — Spending capital on people before validating market

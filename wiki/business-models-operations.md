# Business Models and Startup Operations

How startups make money and function operationally.

## Business Models

### SaaS (Software-as-a-Service)

**How it works**: Charge monthly or annual subscription for access to software

**Pros**:
- Recurring, predictable revenue
- Easy to scale (software costs don't increase with customers)
- Build customer relationships over time
- Can raise VC funding based on MRR/ARR

**Cons**:
- Requires building and maintaining software
- Churn risk (customers can cancel anytime)
- Need to constantly add value to justify price
- Sales cycles can be long (especially enterprise)

**Key metrics**:
- MRR/ARR (monthly/annual recurring revenue)
- Churn rate (% of customers leaving per month)
- CAC (customer acquisition cost)
- LTV (lifetime value)
- Net dollar retention (if customers expand usage)

**Pricing models**:
- Flat fee per user per month
- Tiered pricing (basic, pro, enterprise)
- Usage-based (pay for what you use)

### Marketplace

**How it works**: Connect buyers and sellers, take commission

**Pros**:
- Network effects (more valuable with more users)
- Become essential middleman
- High gross margins

**Cons**:
- Chicken-and-egg problem (need supply and demand)
- Complex operations
- Challenging to scale initially
- Quality control across sides

**Key metrics**:
- GMV (gross merchandise volume) — total transaction value
- Take rate (% commission you keep)
- User growth on both sides
- Conversion (% of visitors who transact)

**Examples**: Uber, Airbnb, DoorDash, eBay

### Creator Economy / Subscription

**How it works**: Creator makes content, fans pay for access

**Pros**:
- Direct relationship with audience
- Recurring revenue
- High margins once scale is reached
- Low operational overhead

**Cons**:
- Creator needs existing audience
- Fickle audiences (can leave easily)
- Need to create constantly
- Platform risk (depends on host platform)

**Examples**: Substack, Patreon, OnlyFans, YouTube

### Advertising

**How it works**: Free product, revenue from ads

**Pros**:
- Free to users (can scale users fast)
- High potential revenue at scale
- Easy to understand

**Cons**:
- Needs millions of users to be profitable
- Incentive misalignment (don't want to optimize for users, optimize for ad revenue)
- Saturated market (very hard to beat incumbents)
- Business only works at massive scale

**Examples**: Google, Facebook, Twitter

### E-commerce

**How it works**: Buy products, sell them for higher price

**Pros**:
- Direct customer relationships
- Can build brand
- Differentiation possible through curation/service

**Cons**:
- Thin margins
- Inventory risk
- Logistics complexity
- Capital intensive

**Examples**: Shopify (platform), Warby Parker (direct-to-consumer)

### Services / Consulting

**How it works**: Sell your time and expertise

**Pros**:
- Quick to start (just start doing)
- Cash flow positive quickly
- Easy to validate (clients will pay)
- Can be high margin

**Cons**:
- Doesn't scale (limited by your time)
- Requires constant selling
- Ceiling on revenue (can only work so much)
- Hard to sell if you leave

**Best approach**: Use services to validate product idea, then transition to product business

### Freemium

**How it works**: Free basic product, paid advanced features

**Pros**:
- Easy user acquisition
- Natural funnel (free users become customers)
- Can sell upgrades to engaged users
- Risk-free trial

**Cons**:
- Need massive free user base to be profitable
- Free users can't sustain
- Need to convert enough to justify free tier
- Requires excellent engagement

**Key metrics**: 
- Conversion rate (% of free to paid)
- Free to paid ratio (typically 5-20% convert)
- Engagement of free users

### Hybrid Models

Most successful startups combine multiple models:
- SaaS + Marketplace (Stripe, Shopify)
- B2B SaaS + Consulting (Salesforce)
- Free product + Premium + Enterprise (Slack)

## Revenue Metrics

### MRR / ARR

**MRR** (Monthly Recurring Revenue) = predictable monthly revenue
**ARR** (Annual Recurring Revenue) = MRR × 12

Important for SaaS and subscription models.

**Calculating MRR**:
- All active subscriptions × average price
- For growing MRR, also track "new MRR" added each month
- Track "churned MRR" (revenue lost to cancellations)

### Burn Rate and Runway

**Burn rate** = monthly cash outflow (how much you spend per month)

**Runway** = current cash / monthly burn rate

**Examples**:
- $500K cash, $50K monthly burn = 10 months runway
- Time to raise next round: plan for 3-6 months
- Safe runway: 12-18 months

**Optimizing runway**:
- Reduce burn (spend less)
- Increase revenue (make more)
- Raise capital (get more runway)

### Gross Margin and Unit Economics

**Gross margin** = (Revenue - Direct Costs) / Revenue

**Direct costs** (COGS): What it costs to deliver your product to one customer
- Server costs for software
- Shipping for e-commerce
- Cost of goods sold for marketplace
- Contractor costs for services

**Why it matters**:
- <40% margin: Hard to scale profitably
- 40-60%: Good for software/SaaS
- >70%: Excellent (software at scale)

**Unit economics**: Profitability of one customer transaction
- Revenue from one customer
- Minus direct costs to serve them
- Should be positive or path to positive

### CAC and LTV

**CAC** (Customer Acquisition Cost):
- Total marketing + sales spend / number of new customers
- Lower is better
- Should be earned back from LTV in 6-12 months

**LTV** (Lifetime Value):
- Total profit from one customer over their lifetime
- For SaaS: (monthly profit × average customer lifetime in months)
- Should be 3-5x CAC for healthy unit economics

**Example**:
- Monthly SaaS price: $100
- Monthly cost to serve: $20
- Monthly profit per customer: $80
- Average customer lifetime: 24 months
- LTV = $80 × 24 = $1,920
- Target CAC: $1,920 / 5 = $384 (should acquire for <$384)

## Operations & Finance

### Financial Planning

**Build financial model**:
- Projections for 3 years
- Revenue assumptions (conservative)
- Cost assumptions (be realistic)
- Cash flow (when do you get paid vs. when do you pay)
- Burn rate and runway

**Update regularly**:
- Monthly actual vs. plan
- Adjust going forward
- Test different scenarios (slow growth, fast growth)

### Cash Management

**Track carefully**:
- Cash in (customer payments, funding, loans)
- Cash out (salaries, tools, operations)
- Forecast (when do you run out?)

**Conserve cash**:
- Don't spend like you have money if you don't
- Negotiate payment terms (net 30 or 60 if possible)
- Pay attention to cash flow, not just revenue
- Revenue ≠ cash (ARR might be owed, not paid yet)

### Legal & Incorporation

**Incorporate early**:
- Protects personal liability
- Enables equity grants to employees
- Required for raising capital

**Structure**:
- C-corp (if raising VC or planning exit)
- LLC (if bootstrapping, simpler)
- S-corp (later, for tax efficiency)

**Get a lawyer early**:
- Incorporation documents
- Founder agreements
- Employee agreements and option plans
- Terms of service and privacy policy
- Investor agreements

### Risk Management

**Key risks**:
- **Cash**: Will you run out?
- **Product**: Does it work? Do customers want it?
- **Market**: Is it big enough? Are you early/late?
- **Team**: Can you execute? Will team stay together?
- **Execution**: Can you build it?

**Mitigation**:
- Validate problem and market before building
- Keep 12+ months runway
- Build diverse team with complementary skills
- Get customer feedback constantly
- Plan for contingencies

### Scaling Checklist

As you grow, add these operational functions:

**First 10 employees**:
- [ ] Financial tracking and bookkeeping
- [ ] HR processes and offer letters
- [ ] Equity management system
- [ ] Team communication and meetings

**10-30 employees**:
- [ ] Department structure and managers
- [ ] Performance review process
- [ ] Benefits and HR policies
- [ ] Defined company values and culture documentation
- [ ] Accounting and tax preparation
- [ ] Customer contracts and legal review

**30+ employees**:
- [ ] CFO or finance person
- [ ] VP/Head of People/HR
- [ ] Formal board and board processes
- [ ] Multiple departments with clear heads
- [ ] Detailed financial forecasting
- [ ] Formal compliance and risk management

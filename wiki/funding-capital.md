# Funding & Capital for Startups

Understanding how to fund your startup and work with investors.

## Overview: Sources of Startup Capital

### 1. Bootstrapping (Self-Funded)
**What it is**: Using your own money, revenue, and resources to fund the company

**Pros**:
- Complete control and ownership
- No pressure to grow beyond what makes sense
- Forced discipline on spending
- Keep all upside

**Cons**:
- Limited capital limits speed
- Personal financial risk
- Harder to hire top talent
- Slower to market

**Best for**: Service businesses, lifestyle businesses, B2B businesses with clear path to revenue

### 2. Friends & Family
**What it is**: Loans or investments from people who know you

**Pros**:
- Easier to get than institutional funding
- More flexible terms
- Relationship-based

**Cons**:
- Mixes money and relationships (risky)
- May lack sophistication on structures
- Limited capital
- Can damage relationships if things fail

**Best for**: Earliest stage, pre-product, before you can raise institutional capital

### 3. Angel Investors
**What it is**: Wealthy individuals who invest their own money in early-stage startups

**Pros**:
- Bring experience and networks
- Flexible with terms
- Can mentor you
- Often faster decision than VCs

**Cons**:
- Give up equity
- Each check is relatively small ($25k-$250k typically)
- Need to convince them individually

**Best for**: Post-MVP, some early traction, before institutional funding

**How it works**:
- Pitch at angel networks (AngelList, local angel groups, accelerators)
- SAFE or convertible note structure (converts to equity in future funding round)
- Typically 10-15% dilution for angel rounds

### 4. Seed Funding (Early VC)
**What it is**: Small venture capital rounds, typically $500k-$3M

**Pros**:
- More capital than angels
- VCs bring expertise and networks
- Can attract other investors and talent
- Structured process

**Cons**:
- Much more dilution (15-25%)
- VCs have expectations for growth
- Long fundraising process (3-6 months)
- VC money comes with pressure to scale

**Best for**: Proven product-market fit signals, ready to scale

**How it works**:
- Pitch to seed funds and early-stage VCs
- Prepare pitch deck (see resources)
- Negotiate terms and valuations
- Typically post-money SAFE or Series Seed equity round

### 5. Venture Capital (Series A, B, C...)
**What it is**: Large capital rounds from institutional investors

**Series A**: $2M-$10M, after PMF, ready to scale
**Series B**: $10M-$30M, proving growth model, expanding
**Series C+**: $30M+, scaling aggressively, preparing for exit

**Pros**:
- Large capital for aggressive growth
- Credibility with customers, talent, partners
- Board seats and governance
- Follow-on funding relationships

**Cons**:
- Significant dilution (20-30% per round)
- Expectation for hockey stick growth
- Board oversight and loss of control
- Eventually need liquidity event (exit)

**How it works**:
- Pitch to VC firms
- Prepare business plan and financials
- Negotiate valuation and terms
- Equity financing rounds

### 6. Debt Financing
**What it is**: Borrowing money that must be repaid

**Types**:
- **Term loans**: Fixed amount, repaid over time
- **Lines of credit**: Access to credit up to limit
- **Revenue-based financing**: Repay as % of revenue

**Pros**:
- Doesn't dilute equity
- Interest is tax deductible
- Can use for specific purposes (equipment, etc.)

**Cons**:
- Must repay regardless of success
- Personal guarantees often required
- Less available for very early stage
- Monthly obligations strain cash flow

**Best for**: Later stage, when revenue is predictable

### 7. Grants & Non-Dilutive Funding
**What it is**: Money that doesn't need to be repaid or equity given

**Types**:
- Government grants (SBA, SBIR, regional programs)
- Corporate grants
- Foundation grants
- Accelerator grants

**Pros**:
- No dilution
- No repayment required
- Free money

**Cons**:
- Competitive and time-consuming
- Restrictions on how to use
- Limited availability
- Often small amounts

**Best for**: Specific industries (cleantech, biotech, defense) or founders from underrepresented groups

## Understanding Valuations

**Valuation** = What the company is worth

### Methods of Valuation

#### 1. Post-Money SAFE
**Safe** = Simple Agreement for Future Equity
- Not a stock purchase, not a loan
- Converts to equity at next funding round
- Investor gets discount (typically 10-30%)
- Valuation cap (maximum valuation for conversion)

**Example**: $500K investment on SAFE with $5M valuation cap
- At Series A fundraising at $10M post-money, safe converts at $5M rate
- Investor gets $500K / $5M = 10% ownership

#### 2. Equity Round
- Investor buys stock directly
- **Pre-money valuation**: Company value before investment
- **Post-money valuation**: Company value after investment
- **Investor ownership** = Investment / Post-money valuation

**Example**: $1M investment at $4M post-money valuation
- Pre-money = $3M
- Investor ownership = $1M / $4M = 25%

### Valuation Frameworks

**For early stage (pre-revenue or early revenue)**:
- 10x multiple on annual revenue (for B2B SaaS)
- Comparables to similar stage companies
- Discounted cash flow (risky to use early)

**For growth stage (proven model)**:
- ARR multiple (e.g., 10-30x ARR for SaaS)
- Revenue growth rate multiples
- Public company comparables

**Venture Capital Method**:
1. Estimate exit value (acquisition price or IPO valuation)
2. Determine required return for investors (10x typical)
3. Work backward to today's valuation

**Valuation Reality**:
- Early stage is largely negotiation — no "right" answer
- Equity is diluted with each round — founders typically 15-20% after Series C
- Valuation matters less than the opportunity and terms

## Fundraising Process

### Before You Start Raising
- Validate business (MVP, some traction)
- Build a cap table (track who owns what)
- Write pitch deck (12-15 slides)
- Prepare financials and model
- Build your story

### The Fundraising Funnel
1. **Sourcing** — Identify 100+ potential investors
2. **Intro/cold outreach** — Get meetings through warm intros
3. **Pitch** — Present to investors
4. **Due diligence** — Investors investigate
5. **Term sheet** — Investor offers term sheet
6. **Negotiation** — Negotiate terms
7. **Closing** — Money transfers, paperwork signed

### Timeline
- Plan for 3-6 months of fundraising
- Parallel tracks with multiple investors
- Don't give up after first "no"
- Rejection is normal — expect 90%+ no's

### Due Diligence
Investors will investigate:
- Cap table and founder equity/options
- Customer conversations (references)
- Legal incorporation and IP
- Financial records
- Contracts and partnerships
- References from current customers/partners

## Key Terms to Understand

**Dilution**: Your ownership percentage decreases as new rounds happen
- Founders often expect 20% ownership post-Series C

**Liquidation preference**: In a down exit, who gets paid first
- 1x non-participating preferred: Get invested amount or gain, not both
- Participating preferred: Get invested amount AND share in gains

**Board seats**: Investor gets seat on company board (governance)

**Anti-dilution**: Investor protection if next round is at lower valuation

**Vesting**: Equity is earned over time (typically 4 years, 1-year cliff)

**Option pool**: 10-20% equity reserved for employee stock options

## Pitching to Investors

### The Elevator Pitch (30 seconds)
"We're building [product] for [customer] to solve [problem]. The market is [size]. We've [traction]. We're looking for $[amount] to [use of funds]."

### The Pitch Deck (12-15 slides)
1. Problem (what's broken)
2. Solution (how you fix it)
3. Market opportunity (how big)
4. Product (what you built)
5. Traction (proof it works)
6. Business model (how you make money)
7. Competition (who else is doing this)
8. Team (who you are)
9. Financials (projections)
10. Why now (market timing)
11. Ask (how much and what for)

### Pitch Tips
- Tell a story (not a recitation of facts)
- Show, don't tell (demonstrate product)
- Be honest about challenges
- Show passion without being over the top
- Practice relentlessly
- Know your numbers cold

## After Fundraising

**What you owe investors**:
- Regular updates (monthly or quarterly)
- Financial information
- Progress toward milestones
- Board meetings (if they have seat)
- Information rights in follow-on rounds

**Managing investor relationships**:
- Be honest about progress and setbacks
- Ask for help on network and expertise
- Don't over-promise on milestones
- Build real partnerships, not just fund-raising relationships

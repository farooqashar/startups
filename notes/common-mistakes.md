# Common Startup Mistakes

Patterns that repeat across failed or struggling startups.

## Before Launch

### Building Without Validation

**Mistake**: Spend months building before talking to customers

**Why it's bad**:
- Building the wrong thing (user feedback shows different needs)
- Misunderstanding the problem (assumptions turn out false)
- Wasting time and money on features nobody wants
- Losing momentum and motivation

**How to avoid**:
- Talk to 20+ potential customers before building anything
- Use wireframes/mockups to validate design before coding
- Launch MVP quickly (6-8 weeks max)
- Get real feedback from real users immediately

### Solving Your Own Problem vs. Market Need

**Mistake**: "I wanted this product, so other people must want it too"

**Why it's bad**:
- Your problem might be unique to you
- You're a terrible judge of what your product should be
- Solution that works for you might not work for 1000 others

**How to avoid**:
- Talk to strangers with the problem (not just friends)
- Validate market size (is this a problem for enough people?)
- Test with people not like you
- Watch people use your product, don't just ask questions

### Picking Wrong Cofounders

**Mistake**: "My friend is awesome, we'll be great cofounders"

**Why it's bad**:
- Co-founder relationships are intense and require trust
- Complementary skills matter as much as being friends
- Different visions or work styles create gridlock
- Failed cofounder relationship kills the startup

**How to avoid**:
- Work with someone before committing
- Discuss expectations explicitly (work style, hours, equity)
- Make sure you have complementary skills
- Have clear agreement on equity split and roles
- Pick people who can say "no" to you

## Early Stage

### No Clear Business Model

**Mistake**: "We'll figure out how to make money later"

**Why it's bad**:
- Hard to validate that business is viable
- Growth in wrong direction (users ≠ revenue)
- Investors won't fund without monetization path
- May never actually achieve profitability

**How to avoid**:
- Know how you'll make money before launch
- Test monetization early (get people to pay)
- Don't need to be profitable early, but need to know path
- Unit economics should make sense (LTV > CAC)

### Wrong Metrics

**Mistake**: Tracking vanity metrics (total signups) instead of real metrics (engagement, retention)

**Why it's bad**:
- Look great but don't tell you if product is working
- Growth that looks good but churns quickly is illusion
- Misallocate resources based on wrong signal

**How to avoid**:
- Retention is the most important early metric
- Track DAU/MAU, not just total users
- Focus on engaged users, not just signups
- Watch cohort retention (do newer users churn faster?)
- Optimize for retention before growth

### Premature Scaling

**Mistake**: Hiring before you have product-market fit

**Why it's bad**:
- Burning cash fast with no path to revenue
- Hard to hire good people without traction
- Team gets in the way (communication overhead)
- Pivots become harder with 20 people than with 5

**How to avoid**:
- Keep team small until you've proven something
- Focus on finding PMF (small team is better)
- Hire for specific needs, not "just in case"
- Growth stage = after PMF, not before

### Ignoring Competition or Markets

**Mistake**: "Our product is so good competitors don't matter" or "Nobody is doing this"

**Why it's bad**:
- Large companies can copy you quickly and cheaply
- If nobody is doing it, maybe the market doesn't want it
- Competition is often a sign market exists
- Competitors' failures can teach you what not to do

**How to avoid**:
- Research competitors thoroughly
- If no competition, validate market carefully
- If big competitors exist, focus on underserved segment
- Learn from competitors but build differently
- Competition validates market (usually good)

## Growth Stage

### Hiring Wrong People

**Mistake**: Hiring fast because you can or need bodies

**Why it's bad**:
- Bad hire is multiple times more expensive than no hire
- Damages culture and team morale
- Dragging out a bad hire is cruel to them and the company
- One bad hire affects everyone

**How to avoid**:
- Hire slowly, fire fast
- Look for cultural fit AND competence
- Do reference checks
- Have team interview and give feedback
- Try people out before committing (contract work, trial period)

### Losing Focus

**Mistake**: Trying to do everything, chasing every opportunity

**Why it's bad**:
- Jack of all trades, master of none
- Spread too thin to do anything well
- Lose competitive advantage in core area
- Team gets confused about priorities

**How to avoid**:
- Define core product and stick to it
- Say no to distractions
- Focus on what differentiates you
- Keep a "no way" list (things you explicitly won't do)
- Review quarterly if focus is right

### Losing Connection with Customers

**Mistake**: Once you have growth, stop talking to customers

**Why it's bad**:
- Lose understanding of what customers want
- Build features nobody asked for
- Product diverges from customer needs
- Competitors step in to serve customers better

**How to avoid**:
- Commit to regular customer research (weekly at minimum)
- Leadership talks to customers
- Measure NPS and track feedback
- Have feedback channels (support, interviews, surveys)
- Bias toward listening, not defending

### Unit Economics Don't Work

**Mistake**: Scaling when LTV < CAC or margins are terrible

**Why it's bad**:
- The more you grow, the more money you lose per customer
- Can't raise enough capital to sustain until you become profitable
- Eventually hit wall (can't afford to acquire customers)
- Scaling broken economics is just accelerating toward failure

**How to avoid**:
- Calculate CAC and LTV before scaling
- Don't scale marketing until unit economics work
- Focus on retention and upsell (improves LTV)
- Reduce CAC through better targeting and product
- Ensure gross margin supports operating costs

## General Patterns

### Founder-Market Fit Issues

**Mistakes**:
- Wrong founder for the market
- Founder doesn't understand customer deeply
- Founder not credible to market (customers don't trust them)
- Founder not willing to do what's needed (sales, partnerships, etc.)

**Solutions**:
- Pick markets aligned with your background and interests
- Deep knowledge of customer helps (experience in industry, lived experience)
- Build credibility through initial customers and proof
- Be willing to do whatever it takes (not just what's fun)

### Not Raising Enough Capital

**Mistakes**:
- Raising just enough for 6 months (doesn't give runway to find PMF)
- Optimistic about timeline to profitability
- No buffer for setbacks

**Solutions**:
- Raise for 12-18 months minimum
- Be pessimistic about timeline
- Build buffer for contingencies
- Plan B: how will you raise more capital?

### Not Managing Burn

**Mistakes**:
- Spending because you have money
- Not tracking burn carefully
- Optimistic revenue projections
- Unexpected cost overruns

**Solutions**:
- Track burn weekly or monthly
- Conservative revenue projections
- Forecast runway constantly
- Have plan if fundraising takes longer

### Team Conflict and Dysfunction

**Mistakes**:
- Unresolved conflict between cofounders
- Unclear roles and responsibilities
- No psychological safety
- Decisions made without input

**Solutions**:
- Address conflict early and directly
- Define clear roles but expect overlap early
- Create culture where people can disagree and be heard
- Make decisions transparently and explain reasoning

### Not Adapting When Data Shows Otherwise

**Mistakes**:
- In love with original plan
- Ignoring data that shows different direction
- "We'll make it work" instead of pivoting
- Overconfidence in original vision

**Solutions**:
- Test hypotheses constantly
- Let data inform direction
- Be willing to pivot
- Distinguish between "we're not there yet" and "this isn't working"
- Set thresholds: "If X metric doesn't hit Y by month Z, we pivot"

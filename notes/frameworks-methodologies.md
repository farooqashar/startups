# Startup Frameworks and Methodologies

Proven frameworks and processes for building and scaling startups.

## Lean Startup Methodology (Eric Ries)

**Core idea**: Test your hypotheses quickly and cheaply. Learn from results. Iterate.

**The Build-Measure-Learn Cycle**:
1. **Build** — Create MVP based on hypothesis
2. **Measure** — Track metrics and customer behavior
3. **Learn** — Extract insights, decide what to do next
4. **Iterate** — Adjust hypothesis and repeat

**Why it works**:
- Reduces time and cost of learning
- Makes decisions based on data, not guesses
- Enables pivoting when needed

**How to use it**:
- Set clear hypotheses before building
- Define success metrics before launch
- Review metrics weekly (don't wait for perfection)
- Make decisions on data, not feelings

## Jobs to Be Done (Clayton Christensen)

**Core idea**: Customers don't want products, they want jobs done. Understanding their job helps you build better products.

**The framework**:
- What "job" are customers trying to accomplish?
- What are they currently using to accomplish it?
- What's frustrating about current solutions?
- What would ideal solution look like?

**Example**: People don't want a 1/4 drill bit. They want a 1/4 hole. Drill bit is how they accomplish the job.

**Why it works**:
- Helps you understand customer motivation
- Prevents solving wrong problem
- Shows why customers switch to competitors
- Helps with positioning and marketing

**How to use it**:
- Interview customers about their job
- Don't ask about product, ask about workflow
- Understand what success looks like to them
- Design solution around the job

## Pirate Metrics (Dave McClure)

**Core idea**: Focus on metrics that matter for growth: Acquisition, Activation, Retention, Revenue, Referral (AARRR).

**The metrics**:
1. **Acquisition** — How do customers find you? (visitors, downloads, signups)
2. **Activation** — What % complete first valuable action? (onboarding completion)
3. **Retention** — Do they come back? (DAU/MAU, churn)
4. **Revenue** — Do they pay? (MRR, ARR, LTV)
5. **Referral** — Do they tell others? (viral coefficient, NPS)

**Why it works**:
- Provides simple framework for metrics
- Shows where your funnel is broken
- Helps prioritize improvements

**How to use it**:
- Plot your metrics in each category
- Identify which is weakest (your bottleneck)
- Focus on improving bottleneck first
- Move to next when bottleneck is solved

## Lean Canvas (Ash Maurya)

**Core idea**: One-page business plan focused on startup-specific concerns (not full business plan).

**The sections**:
1. Problem — What problems are you solving?
2. Solution — How are you solving them?
3. Key Metrics — How will you measure success?
4. Unique Value Proposition — Why you vs. competitors?
5. Unfair Advantage — What can't competitors easily copy?
6. Channels — How do customers hear about you?
7. Customer Segments — Who are you selling to?
8. Cost Structure — What does it cost to run?
9. Revenue Streams — How do you make money?

**Why it works**:
- Forces you to think about full business model
- One page keeps you focused
- Easy to iterate and discuss

**How to use it**:
- Fill out one on day 1 (hypotheses)
- Update as you learn from customers
- Share with investors and advisors
- Reference when making decisions

## Design Thinking

**Core idea**: Use empathy and iteration to solve problems. Emphasis on user perspective.

**The process**:
1. **Empathize** — Understand user deeply (interviews, observation)
2. **Define** — Define the problem from user's perspective
3. **Ideate** — Generate many possible solutions (no judgment)
4. **Prototype** — Build quick, rough versions
5. **Test** — Get feedback from users
6. **Iterate** — Refine based on feedback

**Why it works**:
- Starts from user perspective (not inside-out)
- Encourages exploration (multiple ideas before committing)
- Tests with users early (not assumptions)

**How to use it**:
- User interviews before ideating
- Sketch multiple solutions
- Build prototypes, not finished products
- Share prototypes with real users for feedback

## OKRs (Objectives and Key Results) - John Doerr

**Core idea**: Clear goals with measurable results. Alignment across organization.

**Structure**:
- **Objective** — What do we want to achieve? (qualitative, inspiring)
- **Key Results** — How do we know we succeeded? (3-4 measurable results)

**Example**:
- Objective: "Become the best mobile payment app"
- Key Results:
  - Hit 500k active users (currently at 50k)
  - Achieve 40% D7 retention (currently at 25%)
  - Hit $1M ARR (currently at $100k)

**Why it works**:
- Forces clarity on what matters
- Measurable (you know if you succeeded)
- Alignment (everyone knows goal)
- Flexibility (allows multiple paths to goal)

**How to use it**:
- Set 1-3 OKRs per quarter
- Make them ambitious but achievable
- Review monthly
- Celebrate hitting them or learn from misses

## Agile Development

**Core idea**: Deliver working software in short sprints, get feedback, iterate.

**The process**:
- Sprint (1-2 week iteration)
- Daily standup (quick sync)
- Sprint review (show what was built)
- Retrospective (what went well, what didn't)
- Next sprint planning

**Why it works**:
- Regular delivery shows progress
- Quick feedback loops
- Team alignment through daily syncs
- Continuous improvement through retrospectives

**How to use it**:
- Define story sizes (what can be done in sprint)
- Daily standup (15 min max)
- End of sprint demo (show to stakeholders)
- Monthly review of what's working/not

## North Star Metric (Sean Ellis)

**Core idea**: Focus on one metric that represents product value and business success.

**Examples**:
- Airbnb: Nights booked
- Slack: Messages sent
- Uber: Trips completed
- Netflix: Hours watched
- LinkedIn: Valuable profile views

**Why it works**:
- Prevents metric overload
- Simple to understand and communicate
- Aligns entire company
- Shows if product is delivering value

**How to use it**:
- Pick one metric representing product value
- Build everything toward improving it
- Track trends over time
- Use it to guide roadmap

## Blue Ocean Strategy (W. Chan Kim)

**Core idea**: Don't compete in crowded market (red ocean). Create new market (blue ocean).

**How it works**:
- Red Ocean: Compete on known factors (price, features)
- Blue Ocean: Create new market category
- Requires different value proposition

**Examples**:
- Uber: Created new market (flexible ride-sharing) vs. competing with taxis
- Slack: Created category of team chat (not competing with email)
- Netflix: Created streaming market (not competing with video rental)

**How to use it**:
- Look at your market
- What are competitors competing on?
- What if you ignored that and competed on something else?
- What new customers would that attract?

## Advice for Applying Frameworks

1. **Don't pick all of them** — Pick one or two that fit your situation
2. **Adapt, don't follow exactly** — Use frameworks as guides, not rules
3. **Revisit as you grow** — Early-stage tools differ from growth-stage tools
4. **Communicate in shared language** — If using framework, teach team about it
5. **Measure impact** — Does using this framework improve outcomes?
